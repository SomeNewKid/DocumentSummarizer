# Edward Hyde

Edward Hyde was a mysterious and unsettling figure in Victorian London, known less for his public life than for the fear and disgust he inspired in others. Those who encountered him often struggled to describe exactly what was wrong with him. He was small, pale, and physically unimpressive, yet his presence produced an immediate sense of revulsion. People felt, almost instinctively, that there was something malformed in his character.

Hyde moved through the darker streets of the city with secrecy and confidence. He seemed unconcerned with ordinary respectability, social duty, or the opinions of others. His conduct was impulsive, cruel, and often violent. Unlike many villains who hide behind charm, Hyde appeared to lack even the desire to seem decent. He gave the impression of a person ruled by appetite, anger, and self-interest.

His reputation worsened after several shocking incidents brought him to public attention. Yet much about him remained obscure: his origins, his occupation, and his place in society were all difficult to explain. Hyde is memorable because he represents a frightening idea: not evil as a grand plan, but evil as a liberated impulse, stripped of shame, sympathy, and restraint.
