# Dr Henry Jekyll

Dr Henry Jekyll was a respected London physician and scientist, known among his peers as a man of intelligence, generosity, and social standing. Born into comfort and educated for a learned profession, he moved easily through the drawing rooms and professional circles of Victorian society. His home reflected both his success and his divided interests: part elegant residence, part laboratory, where he pursued private experiments beyond the ordinary work of medicine.

Jekyll had a reputation for kindness and charm. Friends regarded him as warm, thoughtful, and hospitable, though at times they noticed in him a tendency toward secrecy and sudden withdrawal. Beneath his polished exterior, he was deeply concerned with questions of morality, identity, and human nature. He believed that respectable society often forced people to hide parts of themselves, and this belief drove much of his scientific curiosity.

As a doctor, Jekyll was not merely interested in treating illness. He wanted to understand the inner structure of the human character. His ambitions were bold, perhaps too bold, and his private research led him into dangerous territory. He remains one of literature’s most memorable examples of a brilliant person whose search for knowledge outran his wisdom.
